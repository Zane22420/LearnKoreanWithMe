A Tale of Two Wants

(and other such needless poetry)

한극어 has two ways to say "want". They are:

원합니다

and

-고 싶습니다

So, how do you know which to use ? Well that's where the title comes in. One of these:

원헙니다

is ultra poetic. It sourds very literary and dramatic. While we will go over how to use it, it shouldn't be focused on. Unless you're demanding world peace or pining after a lost love, you'll never use it! For more natural sounding korean, you should try to make

-고 싶습니다

work for you by adding the appropriate verb. We'll discuss how to do that in some upcoming sections, but first we have to be familiar with Verb Stems.

What the heck is a verb stem ?

They're not as scary as they sound. They're actually super easy. Have you noticed that our vebrs aways tend to end in -다 ?

입니다

반갑습니다

좋아합니다

If you want the verb stem, just take off the -다! Here are some examples with verbs we already know!

입니다
입니-

반갑습니다
반갑습니-

좋아합니다
좋아합니-

These are our verb stems. They're called stems because, like the stem of a plant, they generally need to be attached to something else. In our case, a new ending. We'll get the first of these in our next section, where we finally cover how to use -고 싶습니다!

-고 싶습니다

With everything we know know, constructing this next phrase is super simple! Just take your verb stem and "plant" it right on the beginning of -고 싶습니다. Remember though, when doing these, take it slowly. Find the verb stem first, and then attach. This will become very easy in time, but trying to rush now will only be frustrating! In this example, we'll use the verb stems we found in the last example.

입니고 십습니다

"(I) want to be..."

반갑습니고 십습니다

"(I) want to meet...

좋아합니고 십습니다

"(I) want to like..."

In the translations, the word "I" is wrapped in parenthesis only because there is no pronoun. Without one, the same sentence could mean "you want", "s/he wants", "They want" ect. I'd recommend staring at that until it feels a little intuitive. Play around with running through the process with our example verbs, and then try a few of your own.

It's also worth mentioning that, at this point, you can totally throw some nouns and pronouns into the sentence if it makes sense.

조극 고양이를 입니고 십습니다

"I want to be a cat"

조극 고양이를 반갑습니고 십습니다

"I want to meet a cats"

조극 고양이를 좋아합니고 십습니다

"I want to like cats"

고양이! CATS! 고양이! CATS! 고양이! CATS! 고양이! CATS!

원합니다

원합니다 is, comparatively, much simpler. It's also nearly useless for conversation. Still, we should know how it works: To use 원합니다, just treat it like any other verb. Take your noun (with 를/을 attached!) and plop in front, as per the S.O.V word order of korean.

조극 컴퓨터를 원합니다

"I want a computer"

조극 점심을 원합니다

"I want lunch"

조극 고양이를 원합니다

"I want cats"

Try not to to use this one, really. All of the above has a connotation that you're talking about your purpose in life and, apart from that last one anyway, these are all totally innapropriote uses. Revert back to using it if it's the only way you know to say something, just know you might get weird looks for it.

Getting more natual sounding 한국어 with

-고 싶습니다

So, based on what we've learned about each of our wants, you might notice that -고 싶습니다 only works with verbs while 원합니다 only works with nouns. This is true. So, how are we supposed to avoid 원합니다 when say simple things like "I want a house" ?

Well, native korean speakers just insert whatever relevant verb that would allow them to use -고 십습니다.

"I want a house" might become "I want to buy a house."

"I want a cat" might become "I want to find a cat."

And so on. It's actually pretty simple to do before translating a sentetence, it just means knowing a variety of verbs is a bit more important early on in korean than in other languages.

Negation of our likes

Unforturnately, the exact way in which these verbs are converted isn't given and isn't clear. As we go through the book and learn more, I'll come back and update this section. In the meantime, it seems we'll have to stick with knowing that the negated form of 원합니다 is

원하지 않습니다

as in "저는 사과를 원하지 않습니다."

while the negated form of -고 싶습니다 is

-고 싶지 읺습니다

as in "고양이를 좋아합니고 않습니다."

You'd have to be a liar to say that last one, but we can't dwell on that; We have to get to our next topic!

A suprise english lesson!

A study of english anyways. Think of the word "only" in english. In particular, of how its meaning differs based on placement in the following example.

"Only I like cats."

"I like only cats"

In the first example, the meanining is unambiguious: Nobody but you likes cats.

There's more to talk about for the second example. Now, it souds bit unnatural, doesn't it ? You'd only say it like that (as opposed to "I only like cats".) if you were really trying to stress that you don't like anything else. That's a good thing to notice though. In english, the connotative rules for the word "only" are that particular emphasis is placed on the word that directly follows. 한극어 has something a lot like this: the particle 만!

The Particle 만

만 and "only" are like twins from different languages. They're almost identical in every way, down to sharing a meaning. 만 means "only". The only difference between their use is that connotative rule: In english, "only" stresses the word that comes after. In 한극어,"만" stresses the word before it. Here's what the above example would look like in korean:

저만 고양이를 좋아합니다.

저는 고양이만  좋아합니다.

You should take particular note of the fact that 만 replaces other particles. There are exceptions, but this is generally always the case for these "word-like" particles, including this next one!

The particle 도

The particle 도 shares all of it's behavior with 만. The only difference is it's meaning: "also"

It's worth specifically mentioning that, like 만, 도 places particular emphasis on the word that it follows. If it is placed on the subject, like so:

저도 고양이를 좋아합니다.

It means "I also" in the sense that "I am also in the group of people that do this thing/share this quality." In this case, You are a part of the group of people who like cats.

In contrast, if 도 follows an object, like this:

저는 고양이도  좋아하니다.

It would mean "I like cats also." in the sense that "Cats are also part of the group of things that fit this sentence." Here, cats are amoung the other things that you like.

There just one last thing we need know before we can understand our conversation!

하지만

하지만 is pretty simple. It means "But" or "However". The only peculiarity to take note of is that it can only be used at the beginning of a sentence. Here's an example of it in action.

저는 개를 좋아합니다. 하지만 고양이도 좋아합니다.

And with that, we have everything we need to understand our conversation and get to some practice! You can use the bar at the top of the sceen to jump back up the conversation! Work slowly and take each rule one at a time!
