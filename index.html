Want + Noun vs. Want + Verb

Korean has two ways to say "want" - Which one you use depends on whether it precedes a verb or a noun.

저는 책을 원합니다

"I want a book."

저는 책을 받고 싶습니다.

"I want to use a book"

The difference between the two is that the first uses just a nown -"I want a book." The second incorporates another verb in the clause- "to use"

Let's go over both of these.

"Want a thing": Noun + (을/를) 원합니다.

"Don't Want a thing": Noun + (을/를) 원하지 않습니다.

When you want to express that you want something, take the noun and attach the Object Maker. Then add 원헙니다

저는 음식을 원헙니다

"I want food"

저는 연필을 원헙니다

"I want a pencil"

The opposite can be expressed using 원하지 않습니다.

저는 야채를 원하지 않습니다.

"I don't want vegetables."

저는 돈을 원하지 않습니다. 사랑을 원합니다.

"I don't want money. I want love."

"I want to do": Verb Stem + 고싶습니다.

"I don't want to do": Verb Stem + 고싶지 않습니다.

When you want to express that you want to do something, take the Verb Stem and attach 고. Then add 싶습니다.

What is a Verb Stem ?

Getting a verb stem is super simple. Just take a verb and remove the 다, and you have the verb stem

하다

-> 하

벌다

-> 벌

막다

-> 막

Some examples for "want to do something:

저는 과일응 먹고 깊습니다.

"I want to eat fruit"

저는 돈을 벌고 싶습니다.

"I want to earn money"

The opposite would be made by using 고 싶지 않습니다.

저는 야채를 먹고 싶지 않습니다.

"I don't want to eat vegetables."

저는 죽고 싶지 않습니다.

"I don't want to die."

저는 웃고 싶지 않습니다.

"I don't want to laugh."

Remember, if you're expressing that you want to do something, use 고 싶습니다. If you're expressing that you want an object, use 원합니다.

Want + Verb: Verb Stem + 고 싶습니다.

Want + Noun: Noun (Don't forget the object particle) + 원합니다.

"I want" as in "Please give me..."

Noun + (을/를) 주세요

In order to request after something politely, say what you want followed by the Object Marker and then add 주세요

돈을 주세요.

"Please give me money."

힌트를 주세요.

"Please give me a hint."

책을 주세요.

"Please give me a book.

Now that we've learned some important grammar rules, let's read over the conversation.

김철수: 저는 아르바이트를 원합니다.

"I want a part-time job."

김영희: 저도 아르바이트를 원합니다.

"I want a part-time job too."

In this sentence, we use the word 저 with the particle 도. Together, they mean "I also" or "Me too"

The Particle 도

Notice how the particle 도 takes the place of 는 in the above sentence. Saying 저도는 would be incorrect. 도 replaces whatever particle would normally be there, (but not all of them, as we'll learn later)

도, like any other particle, follows directly after a word (No Spaces!). It means "also," "even," or "too."

저도 김치를 좋아헙니다.

"I also like kimchi." (Other people might also like kimchi!)

저는 김치도 좋아합니다.

"I also like kimchi" (I might also like other things!)

Take note of the subtle change in meaning based on the placement of the particle. 도 emphasizes the word it follows. Here's an example where that might be important:

저도 자동차를 원합니다.

"I also want a car" (other people might also want a car)

저는 자동차도 원합니다.

"I also want a car" (I might also want other things)

Because 도 is placed after the word "car", it means "I want a car, in addition to whatever else I may want, rather than "I want a car, just like you do" (That would be the first sentence).

Let's continue with the conversation:

김철수: 하지만 일을 워하지 않습니다. 돈만 원합니다.

"But I don't want work. I only want money."

하지만 means "But" or "However. It can only be used at the beginning of a sentence, however; It is not a conjunction.

The Particle 만

만 means "only", and it works almost exactly the same as 도; it replaces particles and it emphasizes the word it follows.

저만 참치를 좋아합니다.

"Only I want tuna" (nobody else wants it)

저는 참치만 좋아합니다.

"I only want tuna" (I don't want anything else)

김영희: 저도 일하고 싶지 않습니다. 게임읗 하고 습니다.

"I also don't want work. I want to play games."

김철수: 저도 게임을 하고 싶습니다. 하지민 돈도 벌고 싶습니다.

"I also want to play games. But I want to earn money too!
